Screening Interview -- There are many different types of interviews, and a few are listed here. In general, you will be asked similar questions in each interview, but it is good to be aware of what each type is. Personal Interview -- This is a one-on-one in-person interview that is longer and more in-depth than the screening interview. This could be your second or first interview for a particular job. Phone Interview -- An interviewer will call you to ask questions. This usually happens for screening interviews or long-distance interviews. Here are some tips:
  • Have written notes and your resume with you.
  • Be in a quiet space without any distractions.
  • Speak energetically.
Group Interview -- Several candidates for the job will be brought in at the same time and asked to participate in a discussion or a task. Here are some pointers:
  • The purpose is to see how you work with others and communicate.
  • Be polite and listen to everyone's ideas.
  • Show leadership, but don’t be domineering. Instead, act as a guide for the discussion.
Task Oriented Interview -- An interviewer may ask you to do a simple task or solve a problem to test your critical thinking or judge your knowledge of a certain topic. Committee Interview -- Multiple people from the company or organization might interview you at the same time. This is overwhelming, but treat it as you would a personal interview! Direct your answer to the person who asked the question, but try to make eye contact with everyone. This type of interview often occurs after a personal interview. _________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________ Doing well on an interview is not about memorizing answers to all of the questions you might be asked. Instead, it is about learning how to react and highlight your strengths. This takes practice! _________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________ Situational/Scenarios This type of interview question usually asks you to describe a time when you did something. It is best to have a couple of situations in mind (leadership roles, challenging projects, etc.). Each answer should focus on the strengths that you can contribute to the job!
  1. Give an example of how you solved a problem in the past.
  2. Give an example of a time you showed leadership and initiative.
  3. Give an example of a time you were able to contribute to a team project.
  4. Have you ever been on a team where someone was not pulling their own weight? How did you handle it?
  5. Tell me about your proudest achievement.
  6. Give me an example of a time when you had to think out of the box.
  7. Describe how you would handle a situation if you were required to finish multiple tasks by the end of the day and there was no way that you could finish all of them.
  8. Describe a difficult work situation or project and how you overcame it.
Offbeat Sometimes employers will ask you odd questions that have nothing to do with the job. If you are asked something that you absolutely do not know, such as #5, do your best to reason through the answer out loud. There are two purposes for offbeat questions: To catch you off guard and see how you handle pressure, and to test your critical thinking skills.
  1. If you ordered a rare hamburger at a business lunch and they brought it to you well-done, how would you handle it?
  2. Should a leader be feared or liked?
  3. What was the last book you read?
  4. On a scale of one to five, rate me as an interviewer.
  5. How many quarters would have to be stacked on top of each other to reach the height of the empire state building?
  6. If you were given one million dollars to fund your best entrepreneurial idea, what would you choose to do?
